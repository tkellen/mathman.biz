Robyn's emails and Don's responses

12/18/00 from Robyn Kozierok ( email: robynk@shore.net )

I wonder if you still remember me.  I was thinking about ways of working with kids in a public school setting using your materials.  My main motivation was my own 7yo son Ryan, whom I knew would enjoy doing it,but who might be reluctant to do additional academic stuff after spending 7 hours a day in school. 

 So I volunteered to take a handful of kids from my son's K-3 class one morning a week and do some "math enrichment" with them.  I didn't use the "c" word as I think it makes grownups nervous. :)  So I have 7 kids, 1st thru 3rd grades (mostly 2nd) for 75 minutes once a week.  The teachers are pleased to have a chance to do review with the remaining kids without boring these guys, and I am pleased that these guys don't have to sit through the unnecessary-for-them review.

  I will tell you up front that I am doing this with no budget, so I won't be able to engage your consulting services or come for a visit, purchase additional workbooks etc. (I already own 1 full set of your materials.)  So the rest of this is just FYI in case you are interested in what we are doing and how it is working out.  But feel free to delete it if you don't have the time.

  I've seen them 3 times and so far we are having fun.  The first two times I didn't really do the calculus activities, but tried to get them "warmed up" -- they played math games and we did some exploring with fractions and cuisenaire rods and playing with towers of Hanoi.  Last week I had several of them working on cookie sharing.  A few tried to find the pattern for the towers of Hanoi, but didn't get very far.

Ryan, who had done one cookie sharing problem at home, did a different one and also did 1/2 + 1/4 + ...  Most of the kids who did the cookie sharing had a lot of trouble figuring out the fraction names for their various pieces.  A few times I had to ask kids to start over and show me what they'd done because they had a pile of different sized pieces in front of them with no idea how big they were. :)  I was surprised by the number of different ways they did the cookie problem.  We did 5 cookies and 3 kids.  First using the non-special scissors.  Ryan gave each kid a whole cookie, cut the other two in thirds, and gave each person 2/3.  I couldn't imagine that anyone would do it any other way, but I saw 1 + 1/2 + 1/6,   3/2 + 2/3,   and I have one first-grader on her way to 3/2 + 4/24  (boy did we work hard to figure out what to name *those* pieces!)

  I think 7 is larger than the optimal size for the group, but I don't want to turn anyone away.  I have been trying to get them to work together sometimes to figure stuff out.  But these are kids who are used to everything coming easy to them, and they tend to just want me to tell them the answers.  I am still working out the right level of hand-holding and leading them versus letting/making them discover things on their own.

  Anyhow, thought you might be interested to hear what we are up to out here in Vermont with your stuff.  I have a math background and have  a true passion for this kind of stuff, and it is great seeing some of that spark in the eyes of young kids.
